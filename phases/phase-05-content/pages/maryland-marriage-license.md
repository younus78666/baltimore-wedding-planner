Getting a Maryland marriage license takes less than an hour in most counties, costs between $65 and $85, and you'll walk out with it the same day you apply. Here's everything you need to know for 2026.

As a Baltimore-based wedding planner, I've walked dozens of couples through this process. It's simpler than most people expect, and Maryland's rules are friendlier than most states. No residency requirement. No waiting period in most counties. You don't even need to live here.

## What Are the Requirements for a Maryland Marriage License?

To get a Maryland marriage license, both applicants must appear in person at a Circuit Court, bring valid photo ID and their Social Security numbers, be at least 18 years old, and neither can be currently married.

**Here's the complete document checklist:**

- Valid government-issued photo ID for both of you (driver's license, passport, or state ID)
- Social Security numbers for both applicants
- If either of you was previously married: a certified copy of the divorce decree or death certificate (photocopies aren't accepted)
- Both of you must be present at the same time. No one can apply on your behalf.

**Age requirements:**
Maryland requires both applicants to be at least 18. In limited cases, 17-year-olds can marry with parental consent, but this varies by county. If you're both 18 or older, there's nothing extra to worry about.

**Residency:**
Zero residency requirement. You don't need to live in Maryland, work in Maryland, or have any connection to the state. Couples from Virginia, DC, Pennsylvania, and out of the country all apply here regularly. It's one of the most accessible states on the East Coast for this reason.

**Prior marriages:**
If either of you has been married before, you'll need to bring documentation. A certified divorce decree showing the divorce is final, or a certified death certificate if your previous spouse passed away. Circuit Court clerks are strict about this. The document has to be certified, not a photocopy.

## Where Do You Apply for a Maryland Marriage License?

You apply at any Maryland Circuit Court, regardless of where you live or where the ceremony will take place.

**Most couples choose based on convenience:**

- **Baltimore City** — Courthouse East, 111 N. Calvert St. Open Mon-Fri 8:30am-4:30pm. Walk-ins welcome.
- **Annapolis (Anne Arundel County)** — 8 Church Circle, Annapolis. Good option for elopements since staff are used to same-day ceremony requests.
- **Towson (Baltimore County)** — 400 Washington Ave. Convenient for couples in the northern suburbs.
- **Rockville (Montgomery County)** — 50 Maryland Ave. Remember the 48-hour wait if you're using this court.

You don't need an appointment at most Maryland Circuit Courts for marriage license applications. Walk-in and same-day service is the norm.

## Is There a Waiting Period for a Maryland Marriage License?

Most Maryland counties have no waiting period. You can apply in the morning and get married the same afternoon.

Baltimore City, Anne Arundel, Howard, Frederick, Carroll, Harford, Cecil, Talbot, Wicomico, and St. Mary's counties all issue licenses immediately after you apply.

**The two exceptions:**
Montgomery County and Prince George's County require a 48-hour waiting period between when you apply and when the license is issued. If you're planning a ceremony in the DC suburbs, you'll need to factor in that two-day gap.

**Practical tip:** If you're in Montgomery or Prince George's County but your ceremony is flexible on location, you can apply at any other Maryland Circuit Court instead. Apply in Anne Arundel or Howard County and skip the wait entirely.

## How Long Is a Maryland Marriage License Valid?

A Maryland marriage license is valid for six months from the date it's issued. Your ceremony must happen within that window.

After the ceremony, your officiant signs the license. You then have 60 days to file the signed license back with the issuing Circuit Court. Don't wait too long on this step. A signed license that doesn't get filed is a legal problem you don't want.

**What if the license expires?**
If six months pass before you hold the ceremony, the license expires and you'll need to apply again. Same process, same fee. This doesn't happen often, but it's worth knowing if your wedding timeline shifts.

## What Happens After You Get the License?

The license is a document your officiant uses during the ceremony. Here's what the process looks like from license to legal marriage:

1. Apply at the Circuit Court and receive your license
2. Hold your ceremony with a registered officiant and at least one witness present
3. Your officiant signs the license during or immediately after the ceremony
4. You or your officiant returns the signed license to the issuing Circuit Court within 60 days
5. The court records the marriage and it's officially recognized in Maryland and nationally

**Who can be your officiant?**
Maryland accepts: judges, justices of the peace, licensed religious ministers or clergy, and online-ordained ministers. Universal Life Church ordinations are valid in Maryland. Your officiant's name goes on the license, and they sign it at the ceremony.

**How many witnesses do you need?**
One witness is required. Most couples bring two to keep things simple. The witness signs the license at the ceremony.

## Can You Apply for a Maryland Marriage License Online?

No. Maryland requires both applicants to appear in person at a Circuit Court. There's no online application, no mail-in option, and no way to apply separately. Both of you need to walk in at the same time.

This is worth knowing if you're planning a quick elopement from out of state. Build the Circuit Court visit into your travel plans. Most appointments take 20 to 30 minutes, and you'll leave with the license in hand.

## What Documents Do You Need If You Were Previously Married?

If either of you has been married before, you need to bring a certified copy of the document that ended that marriage.

**For a divorce:** Bring the certified copy of the final divorce decree. This is the document issued by the court, not a photocopy of the original. It needs to show that the divorce is final (not pending).

**For a widowed spouse:** Bring the certified death certificate of the former spouse.

Maryland Circuit Court clerks won't accept photocopies for prior marriage documentation. If your divorce was finalized in another state, you'll need to contact that state's court to obtain a certified copy before you can apply in Maryland.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Maryland Marriage Licenses

**Can a friend legally officiate our wedding in Maryland?**
Yes. Any friend or family member who's ordained through a recognized organization can legally perform your Maryland wedding ceremony. Universal Life Church ordinations are valid. Your friend needs to be listed on the license as the officiant and sign it at the ceremony. Check with your specific county's Circuit Court a couple weeks before the wedding just to confirm, since interpretation can vary slightly by county.

**Do both of us need to apply at the same Circuit Court on the same day?**
Yes. Both applicants must appear together at the same Circuit Court at the same time. You can't apply separately, on different days, or through a representative. It takes 20 to 30 minutes and you'll get the license the same day in most counties.

**Can we get a Maryland marriage license if we live in another state?**
Absolutely. Maryland has no residency requirement. Couples from Virginia, DC, Pennsylvania, New York, or anywhere else in the country can apply at any Maryland Circuit Court. The license is then valid for a ceremony anywhere in Maryland.

**What if one of us lost our divorce paperwork?**
You'll need to obtain a certified copy from the court that handled the divorce. If the divorce was in Maryland, contact that county's Circuit Court. If it was in another state, contact that state's court. Most courts can provide certified copies by mail, though processing time varies.

**Is a Maryland marriage license recognized in other states?**
Yes. A properly filed Maryland marriage license is recognized in all 50 states and federally. Once the officiant signs it and it's filed with the Circuit Court, your marriage is legally recognized everywhere in the United States.
