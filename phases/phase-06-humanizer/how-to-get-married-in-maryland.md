## INTRO

Maryland has no residency requirement, no waiting period in most counties, and a marriage license that starts at $65. For couples who want the most straightforward path to a legal marriage on the East Coast, it's hard to pick a better state.

As a Baltimore-based wedding planner, I walk couples through the Maryland marriage license process every month. Some are planning a 120-person reception in the city. Others want a quiet elopement at Quiet Waters Park in Annapolis. The legal requirements are the same either way. This guide covers every step.

## REQUIREMENTS

Getting legally married in Maryland comes down to four things: both people must be at least 18, you have to appear together at a Maryland Circuit Court, you need a valid marriage license, and your ceremony must be performed by a registered or ordained officiant. One witness needs to be present.

Eligibility:
- Both applicants need to be 18 or older
- Neither of you can be currently married
- No Maryland residency required. Out-of-state and international couples are fully eligible.
- First-cousin marriages aren't permitted in Maryland

Documents you'll need at the Circuit Court:
- Valid photo ID for both of you (driver's license, passport, or state ID)
- Social Security numbers for both applicants
- A certified divorce decree or death certificate if either of you was previously married (photocopies aren't accepted)
- Both of you must show up at the same time. No exceptions.

Recognized officiant types:
- Judges and magistrates
- Justices of the Peace
- Licensed religious ministers or clergy
- Online-ordained ministers. Universal Life Church ordinations are valid in Maryland.

## STEP BY STEP

Getting a Maryland marriage license takes under an hour at most Circuit Courts. Most counties hand it to you the same day.

Step 1: Gather your documents. Both applicants need a valid photo ID, their Social Security number, and (if previously married) a certified copy of the divorce decree or death certificate. Photocopies of prior marriage documentation aren't accepted.

Step 2: Pick your Circuit Court. You can apply at any Maryland Circuit Court regardless of where you live or where the ceremony will be. Baltimore City Circuit Court is the most central option for Baltimore-area couples. Anne Arundel County Circuit Court in Annapolis is a solid choice for elopements since they're used to same-day requests.

Step 3: Show up together. Both applicants have to visit the same Circuit Court at the same time. You can't apply separately or send someone in your place.

Step 4: Fill out the application and pay the fee. The clerk processes everything in about 20 to 30 minutes. Most courts take cash and credit cards. Fees run $65 to $85 depending on the county.

Step 5: Get your license. Most Maryland counties issue it the same day. As of 2026, Montgomery and Prince George's counties require a 48-hour wait, so call ahead before booking a same-day ceremony in those areas.

Step 6: Hold your ceremony. Your officiant signs the license during or right after the ceremony. From there, you have 60 days to file the signed license with the Circuit Court.

## WAITING PERIOD

Most Maryland counties have no waiting period at all. Baltimore City, Anne Arundel, Howard, Frederick, Harford, Carroll, and most rural counties issue licenses right after you apply. You can get married the same day.

Montgomery and Prince George's counties require 48 hours between application and issuance. If your ceremony is in the DC suburbs, plan to apply two days before the wedding date.

The license stays valid for six months. After the ceremony, you've got 60 days to file the signed license back with the Circuit Court.

## ELOPEMENT

A legal Maryland elopement needs the same marriage license as any other wedding, a registered officiant, and one witness. The license itself doesn't require a special permit. Whether you need one for your ceremony location depends on where you're planning to hold it.

Six Maryland elopement locations I recommend most often:
- Quiet Waters Park, Annapolis: waterfront South River views, $50 to $100 permit, available year-round
- Patterson Park, Baltimore City: historic pagoda backdrop, no permit needed for groups under 25 people
- Assateague Island National Seashore: Atlantic Ocean beach ceremony, National Park permit $150
- Cunningham Falls State Park, Thurmont: Maryland's largest waterfall, permit through Maryland DNR
- Annapolis City Dock: public waterfront with sailboat views, no permit needed for groups under 10
- Oregon Ridge Park, Baltimore County: wooded meadow and small lake, county permit $75

## FAQ

Can a friend officiate a wedding in Maryland?
Yes, and it's simpler than most people expect. Any friend or family member can legally perform a Maryland wedding ceremony if they're ordained through a recognized organization. Universal Life Church ordinations are valid here. The ordained person needs to be listed on your marriage license as the officiant and sign it right after the ceremony. Confirm with your specific county's Circuit Court a couple weeks before the wedding date, just to be safe.

How long is a Maryland marriage license valid?
Six months from the date it's issued. The ceremony has to happen within that window. After the ceremony, you've got 60 days to file the signed license with the Circuit Court. If the license expires before you get married, you'll need to apply again and pay the fee a second time.

Can you apply for a Maryland marriage license online?
No. Maryland still requires both of you to show up in person at a Circuit Court to apply. There's no online option or mail-in process in any county as of 2026.

Do you need to be a Maryland resident to get married in Maryland?
Not at all. Maryland has zero residency requirement for marriage licenses. Out-of-state couples, international couples, and same-sex couples from states with stricter laws can all walk into any Maryland Circuit Court and apply. You can hold the ceremony anywhere in the state.

How soon after getting the license can you get married?
In most counties (Baltimore City, Anne Arundel, Howard, Frederick, Carroll, Harford) you can get married the same day you receive your license. Montgomery and Prince George's counties require a 48-hour wait. Confirm with the specific Circuit Court before booking your officiant or venue.
